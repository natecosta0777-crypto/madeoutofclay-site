Reading Together: Why Family Storytime Still Matters

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

Here's a number that should give every parent pause: children who are read aloud to for fifteen minutes a day enter kindergarten having heard roughly 1.4 million more words than children who aren't. That gap shows up in vocabulary, comprehension, and the kind of imaginative flexibility that makes learning easier across the board.

But the research only tells part of the story. What the numbers don't capture is what happens in the room — the specific quality of attention that comes from two or three people looking at the same thing, at the same time, without anywhere else to be.

Why it still matters in a screen-first world

The argument for reading aloud often gets framed as a fight against screens. That's not quite right. The issue isn't that screens are bad — it's that reading together does something screens don't: it requires presence that goes both ways. When a parent reads to a child, neither party is passive. The child is tracking the story, anticipating, asking. The parent is performing — choosing voices, pausing, noticing what lands. That mutual attention is increasingly rare and increasingly valuable.

At Sunny's Table is built for this kind of read-aloud. Scooter, the youngest in the sloth family, is always waiting — for spring, for quiet, for his turn. Those books are written to pause. They create natural moments where a parent can look up from the page and ask: Have you ever felt like that? And then the conversation that comes out of that question is the whole point.

A few things that actually help

Same time every day. The ritual matters as much as the reading. When storytime is predictable, it becomes something children anticipate and protect — they'll remind you when you forget, which is its own kind of gift.

Read slower than you think you need to. Picture books are designed to breathe. The illustrations carry part of the story, and children need time to look at them. Let them.

Don't skip the hard questions. Good children's books surface things like jealousy, fear, disappointment, and the complicated feelings that come with being small in a big world. When those moments come up in the story, they're an invitation — not a detour. Take it.

You don't have to finish the book. Sometimes a three-year-old has a question on page four that takes fifteen minutes to work through. That's not a failure to complete the book. That's the book doing its job.

Start tonight

You don't need a plan. You need a book and fifteen minutes. The rest takes care of itself.
