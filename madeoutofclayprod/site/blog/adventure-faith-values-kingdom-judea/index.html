Kingdom of Judea Series

Adventure, Faith, and Values: Inside the Kingdom of Judea Series

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

The Kingdom of Judea series began with a question: what if the most important values — loyalty, courage, faithfulness, the willingness to protect what you love — were taught not through moral instruction, but through adventure? What if a child could absorb them without knowing they were being taught?

The answer is three books, a lion kingdom called Judea, a ridge called Hebron, a valley called Vine Hills, and a cub named Caleb who is still figuring out what it means to belong somewhere.

The world of the series

Judea is a land — and Vine Hills is a valley within it. This distinction matters because the series is partly about the relationship between home as a place and home as a feeling. Caleb knows what Judea looks like. He knows the terrain of Hebron Ridge. But knowing a place and belonging to it are different things, and the series tracks Caleb's journey toward the second kind of knowing.

The cubs have names that carry weight: Caleb, Ezra, Asher. The adversaries in the series echo older stories — Korah, Dathan, Tavin — but these are children's books, and the conflicts are rendered at the scale of young animals learning what loyalty costs and what betrayal feels like. The echo is subtle. The story doesn't require any knowledge of Numbers 16 to work. But for families who carry that knowledge, the resonance is there.

Faith carried lightly

The Kingdom of Judea is faith-infused, but not explicitly religious in the way that might feel prescriptive to families of different traditions. The faith is in the texture of the world: in the way characters speak about what they owe each other, in the weight they give to promises, in the way courage is framed not as the absence of fear but as the decision to act despite it.

Families who want faith-forward children's books will find what they're looking for here. Families who don't will find a good adventure story with well-drawn characters who care deeply about right and wrong. Both readings work.

The three books

Caleb's Homeland establishes the world and the question at the center of the series: what makes a place yours? Caleb is young enough that Judea is the only home he's known, but old enough to sense that something is at stake in keeping it.

The Long Walk is the journey book — the middle of the series in every sense. Something has been lost, and recovering it requires a trek that tests every cub in a different way. The walk is literal and not.

Asher and Lookout Rock shifts the center of gravity to Asher — the quieter cub, the one who watches. Lookout Rock is where you go when you need to see clearly. The book is about what you do with what you see.
