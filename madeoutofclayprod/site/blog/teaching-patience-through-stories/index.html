Teaching Patience to Young Children Through Stories

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

There's a moment in Scooter Can't Wait for Spring where Scooter, the youngest sloth in the family, is standing at the window watching the bare trees, willing something to happen. He's not throwing a tantrum. He's not asking for the fifth time when spring is coming. He's just standing there, full of anticipation that has nowhere to go yet.

Every child knows exactly how that feels. And for most of them, nobody has ever named it quite so clearly.

Why abstract lessons don't work with young children

Telling a four-year-old to "be patient" is approximately as useful as telling them to "be tall." Patience isn't a switch they can flip — it's a regulatory capacity that develops slowly across childhood and adolescence, and it develops through experience and modeling, not instruction.

What stories can do — what direct instruction cannot — is give children a character to identify with who is going through the same thing they go through, and then show them what happens next. Not a resolution that arrives magically, but a process: the discomfort of waiting, the small coping moves a character makes, the eventual arrival of whatever was waited for, and the way it feels different when it finally comes.

What the At Sunny's Table series does differently

The sloth family at the center of this series was chosen deliberately. Sloths are creatures built for a different pace than the world around them. In a children's book context, that becomes a feature — Scooter and his family operate at their own speed, and the stories honor that rather than treating it as a problem to fix.

Sunny, the father, is the model of patient presence in every book. He doesn't rush. He doesn't fix. He waits until the right moment, and then he speaks — and what he says tends to land because of the timing as much as the content. Children absorb this. They may not articulate it, but they're learning what a patient, present adult looks like.

How to use these books as a conversation starter

After reading, try one of these prompts:

"What was Scooter waiting for?" — ground the child in the story before moving to their own experience.

"Have you ever waited for something like that?" — the bridge from the story to their life.

"What did it feel like when it finally came?" — this is where the emotional vocabulary gets built.

You don't need to lecture. The book already did the work. Your job is just to open the door.
