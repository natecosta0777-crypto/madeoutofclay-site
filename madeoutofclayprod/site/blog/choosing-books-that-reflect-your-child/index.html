How to Choose Children's Books That Reflect Your Child

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

Representation in children's books has become a more visible conversation in the last decade. That's good. But the version of that conversation that gets the most attention tends to focus on a narrow slice of representation — racial and ethnic identity, primarily — while leaving out a much broader range of ways a child might look at a book and not see themselves.

Family structure. Neurology. Temperament. The emotional landscape of being the youngest or oldest. The experience of waiting for something, or struggling with something that other kids seem to do easily. All of these are forms of recognition, and children need books that offer all of them.

What to actually look for

  • Does the main character feel something your child feels? Not look like your child — feel like your child. A book about a character who moves too fast, or waits too long, or can't stand loud noises, or desperately wants to be like their older sibling — these land differently than a book about a character who merely shares demographic features.
  • Is the character's experience treated as the whole story? Or is it background noise for someone else's arc? A neurodivergent character who exists to teach the neurotypical classmates a lesson is not a book about that character.
  • Does the book end somewhere true? Not every book needs a happy ending, but it needs an honest one. A book that resolves too easily, or too painfully, leaves children stranded.
  • Does the grown-up figure model something worth modeling? Children absorb what the adults in their stories do. A father who listens before speaking. A mother who names a feeling without minimizing it. These matter.

How our three series approach this

At Sunny's Table is for children who live inside big feelings about small things — waiting for spring, needing quiet, wanting to be exactly like someone else. The emotional register is very specific, and that specificity is the point. If your child is the kind of person who feels things deeply about things that don't seem big from the outside, these books were written for them.

Just the Way You Are is for neurodivergent children and the families who love them. Every book in this series is written from inside the neurodivergent experience — not as an explanation for neurotypical observers, but as a recognition for the children themselves.

The Kingdom of Judea is for children who need adventure, who are drawn to questions of loyalty and courage and what it means to belong somewhere. It's faith-adjacent without being exclusionary. If your child loves a story with real stakes and characters who have to choose, this is that story.

A final note

The best children's book you will ever find for your child is the one they ask for again. Pay attention to that. The repeated request is the child telling you something is true in there. Follow it.
