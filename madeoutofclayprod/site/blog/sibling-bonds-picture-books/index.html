Family & Siblings

The Sibling Stories That Changed How We Write

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

Siblings are not background characters. They're the first peers, the first rivals, the first people who know you before you know yourself — and the relationship between them shapes children in ways that last decades. Somehow, children's books often reduce siblings to plot devices: the jealous older one who learns to share, the adored baby who disrupts everything, the best friend who happens to live in the same house.

We wanted something more complicated. And more honest.

Just Like My Brother

Just Like My Brother is the third book in the At Sunny's Table series, and it's the one that required the most rewrites. The premise is simple: one of the younger sloths wants to be exactly like their older sibling — every gesture, every habit, every way of moving through the world. It's a story about admiration, which is easy. What made it hard was writing the older sibling honestly — not as an idealized figure to be looked up to, but as someone who is also still becoming, who finds the copying both touching and quietly suffocating.

We wanted both feelings in the same book. That's where the story lives.

When one sibling is neurodivergent

The Just the Way You Are series handles sibling dynamics differently — because in families where one child is neurodivergent, the sibling experience is often invisible. The neurotypical sibling may feel overlooked, or guilty for feeling overlooked, or confused about why things are different, or fiercely protective. All of those are true simultaneously and children feel all of them at once.

We've tried to hold that complexity in the books where siblings appear. Not to resolve it — there's no resolution to the complexity of loving someone whose needs are different from yours — but to name it clearly enough that a child reading it feels less alone in feeling it.

Brotherhood in the Kingdom of Judea

In the Kingdom of Judea series, the bonds between the lion cubs — Caleb, Ezra, and Asher — carry the emotional weight of brotherhood without ever using the word. They protect each other, they compete, they misunderstand each other at crucial moments, they find each other again. It's the oldest story there is, and we tried to tell it like it was new.

What children take from these stories

When children read about siblings who feel the full range of what siblings feel — jealousy, pride, protectiveness, rivalry, love that doesn't need to be earned — they get something they don't always get in real life: permission. Permission to want more than what they're getting. Permission to love someone and be furious at them at the same time. Permission to be more than one thing at once.

That's the whole job of a good children's book. These are our best attempts at it.
