Reading With a Child Who Has ADHD: What Actually Works

By Isaiah Hartwell · June 2026

If you've tried to read to an ADHD child and found yourself three minutes in, on the wrong page, having a full conversation about why dinosaurs went extinct and whether a T-Rex could beat a shark — first of all, you're doing it right. Second of all, here's what we've learned about making storytime sustainable (and actually fun) when a child's attention moves faster than the story.

Reframe what "successful" reading looks like

Reading with an ADHD child is not going to look like the reading you see in children's book illustrations — the serene parent, the rapt child, the soft lamp. It's going to be more like a conversation that keeps getting interrupted by the conversation. That's not failure. That's engagement.

The goal is connection through story, not completion of story. A book you read three pages of together, with six tangents, is a better reading session than a book you powered through while your child's brain was somewhere else entirely.

What actually helps

  • Shorter books, read more often. Picture books are the format. Twenty pages of high-quality illustration and deliberately paced text are built for shorter attention spans — that's not a bug, it's the design.
  • Movement is fine. Let a child lie on their stomach, fidget with something, rock slightly. Body movement often helps ADHD brains stay cognitively present, not less. The goal is the ears and imagination, not stillness.
  • Follow the tangents. When your child goes off-book, come back to the page — but don't rush it. The tangent is often where the real processing happens.
  • Read the same book repeatedly. Repetition builds mastery. An ADHD child who asks for the same book twenty times is building something — let them.
  • Books where the character moves fast too. ADHD children respond to characters whose inner experience matches theirs. Clay Has Amazing Powers was written specifically for this — Clay's mind moves at speed, and the story honors it.
